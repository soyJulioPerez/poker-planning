In Thinking in Bets, former professional poker champion Annie Duke argues that life is much less like chess—where there is no hidden information and very little luck—and much more like poker. Every decision we make is ultimately a bet based on incomplete information and a heavy dose of uncertainty.
​Here is the core breakdown of how shifting your mindset from "seeking certainty" to "thinking in bets" can radically improve your decision-making.

## 1. The Trap of "Resulting"

​The biggest mistake people make is evaluating the quality of a decision based solely on its outcome. Duke calls this resulting.
​If you run a red light and make it across safely, it doesn't mean running the light was a smart decision.
​If a CEO makes a highly calculated, intelligent investment that fails due to an unpredictable market crash, it doesn't mean it was a foolish decision.
Separating luck from skill is the first step to clear thinking.

## ​2. "Wanna Bet?" Shifting to Probabilities

​When we say "I'm 100% sure," we set ourselves up for failure. Our brains are hardwired to see the world in black and white right or wrong. Duke suggests asking yourself, "Wanna bet?" This mental trick forces you to realize you are wagering your time, money, or reputation on a belief. Instead of declaring something as a certainty, start phrasing your beliefs in percentages (e.g., "I'm 70% sure this project will hit its deadline"). This makes you more objective and open to updating your views when new data arrives.

## ​3. Overcoming Self-Serving Bias

​We love taking credit for our wins and blaming luck for our losses.
​If we win: It's because of our brilliant strategy.
​If we lose: It was a freak accident, bad luck, or someone else's fault.
To grow, you have to flip this around. Analyze your wins to see if you just got lucky, and dissect your losses to see if there was a flaw in your process.

## ​4. Designing Your Future Self

​Duke offers two practical tools to counteract emotional, short-sighted decisions:

### ​The 10-10-10 Rule:

Ask yourself how you will feel about this decision in 10 minutes, 10 months, and 10 years. This breaks the grip of temporary emotions.

### ​Pre-Mortems:

Before launching a project, imagine it has utterly failed a year from now. Work backward to figure out why it failed. This helps you spot vulnerabilities before they happen.

## ​The Takeaway

You cannot control the cards you are dealt, nor can you control how the wind blows. You can only control the quality of your decisions. By embracing uncertainty and treating decisions as calculated wagers, you stop chasing perfection and start mastering strategy.
